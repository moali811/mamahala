## Section 1: Understanding Imposter Syndrome

### What It Is

Imposter syndrome is the persistent feeling that you are not as competent as others perceive you to be. Despite evidence of your achievements, you attribute your success to luck, timing, or other people's help rather than your own ability.

### The Five Types

1. **The Perfectionist:** You set impossibly high standards. When you fall short, even slightly, you feel like a failure.
2. **The Expert:** You feel you need to know everything before you can speak up. Not knowing something feels like proof of inadequacy.
3. **The Natural Genius:** If something does not come easily, you assume you are not good at it. Struggle feels like evidence of fraud.
4. **The Soloist:** Asking for help feels like admitting weakness. You believe you should be able to do everything on your own.
5. **The Superhuman:** You push yourself to work harder than everyone else to prove you deserve your place.

### Which Type Are You?

Most people identify with one or two types more strongly. Knowing your type helps you target your strategies.

## Section 2: Where It Comes From

### Family Messages

Were you praised for being "the smart one" or "the responsible one"? These labels, while well-intentioned, can create pressure to maintain an identity that feels fragile.

### Cultural Expectations

If you come from a collectivist culture, standing out or claiming individual achievement may feel uncomfortable. If you are a first-generation student or professional, you may feel like you are representing your entire family or community.

### Systemic Factors

Imposter syndrome is not always internal. If you are in spaces where people who look like you are underrepresented, the environment itself can amplify feelings of not belonging.

## Section 3: Strategies That Work

### Strategy 1: Name It

When imposter syndrome shows up, call it out: "This is imposter syndrome talking, not reality." Naming the experience reduces its power.

### Strategy 2: Collect Evidence

Keep a running list of your accomplishments, positive feedback, and moments where you genuinely contributed. When imposter feelings arise, review this list. It is hard to argue with evidence.

### Strategy 3: Reframe the Narrative

Instead of "I got lucky," try "I was prepared and I took the opportunity."
Instead of "Anyone could have done this," try "I did this, and that matters."
Instead of "They will find out I do not belong," try "I am learning and growing, like everyone else."

### Strategy 4: Normalize Struggle

Struggle is not evidence of fraud. It is evidence of growth. Every expert was once a beginner. Every successful person has failed multiple times. Your learning curve is normal.

### Strategy 5: Talk About It

Imposter syndrome thrives in silence. When you share your feelings with a trusted friend, mentor, or counselor, you almost always discover that others feel the same way.

## Section 4: The Imposter Syndrome Journal

### Weekly Check-In

Answer these questions each week:

1. **What did I accomplish this week?** (List at least three things, no matter how small)
2. **What did imposter syndrome tell me this week?** (Write down the exact thoughts)
3. **What is the evidence against those thoughts?**
4. **What would I say to a friend who told me they felt this way?**
5. **What is one thing I will do next week despite feeling like a fraud?**
