## Section 1: What Anger Actually Is

Anger is one of the six basic human emotions. It is hardwired into your brain as a survival mechanism. When you perceive a threat, whether physical or emotional, your brain activates the fight-or-flight response. Your heart rate increases, your muscles tense, and adrenaline floods your system.

### Common Anger Triggers for Teens

- Feeling disrespected or dismissed by adults
- Being compared to siblings or peers
- Academic pressure and fear of failure
- Social exclusion or betrayal by friends
- Family conflict or tension at home
- Feeling misunderstood or not listened to
- Injustice, whether personal or in the world

### The Anger Iceberg

Anger is almost always a surface emotion. Underneath it, you will usually find something more vulnerable: hurt, fear, embarrassment, sadness, loneliness, or frustration. When you can name the emotion under the anger, you are halfway to managing it.

## Section 2: Know Your Anger Pattern

Everyone has an anger pattern. Understanding yours gives you the power to interrupt it before it escalates.

### Step 1: Identify Your Warning Signs

Physical signs might include:

- Clenched jaw or fists
- Tight chest or stomach
- Feeling hot, especially in your face and neck
- Faster breathing or heartbeat
- Restlessness or wanting to pace

### Step 2: Map Your Escalation

Think of anger on a scale of 1 to 10:

| Level | What It Feels Like | What You Might Do |
| ----- | ------------------- | ----------------- |
| 1-3 | Irritated, annoyed | Sigh, eye-roll, short answers |
| 4-6 | Frustrated, heated | Raise voice, argue, withdraw |
| 7-8 | Furious, out of control | Yell, slam things, say hurtful words |
| 9-10 | Explosive, seeing red | Physical aggression, total shutdown |

Your goal is to catch yourself at 3-4 and use a strategy before you reach 7+.

## Section 3: Five Strategies for the Moment

### Strategy 1: The 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding Technique

When anger is rising, ground yourself in the present moment:

- **5** things you can see
- **4** things you can touch
- **3** things you can hear
- **2** things you can smell
- **1** thing you can taste

This pulls your brain out of fight mode and back into the present.

### Strategy 2: The Exit Line

Have a pre-planned sentence ready for when you need to walk away:

- "I need a few minutes. I will come back when I am calmer."
- "I am too upset to talk about this right now. Can we pause?"

Walking away is not weakness. It is strategy.

### Strategy 3: Cold Water Reset

Splash cold water on your face or hold ice cubes in your hands. This activates your body's dive reflex and physically slows your heart rate. It sounds simple because it is.

### Strategy 4: Physical Release

Anger lives in your body. Move it through your body:

- Go for a run or fast walk
- Do push-ups or jumping jacks
- Hit a pillow or punch a mattress
- Squeeze a stress ball as hard as you can

### Strategy 5: Write It Out

Grab paper and write everything you are feeling. Do not censor yourself. Do not worry about grammar or making sense. Just get it out. You can tear it up after. The goal is transfer: moving the anger from inside you to outside you.

---

## Section 4: After the Storm

### Repair Matters

If your anger hurt someone, you can repair it. A genuine apology has three parts:

1. **Name what you did:** "I yelled at you and said something hurtful."
2. **Acknowledge the impact:** "That was not okay and I understand it hurt you."
3. **State what you will do differently:** "Next time I am that angry, I am going to walk away first."

### Track Your Progress

You will not go from explosive to zen overnight. Progress looks like catching yourself earlier on the scale, using one strategy instead of none, and repairing faster after a blow-up.
