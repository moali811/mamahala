## A Note from Dr. Hala

Dear teen,

The question "Who am I?" is one of the most important questions you will ever ask. And the truth is, you do not need to have a final answer. Identity is not a destination. It is a journey that unfolds over your entire life.

But right now, during your teen years, this question feels especially urgent. You are navigating peer pressure, cultural expectations, family dynamics, and your own evolving sense of self. That is a lot.

## Section 1: The Layers of Identity

Your identity is not one thing. It is many layers, and they interact with each other in complex ways.

### Your Identity Wheel

Think about each of these dimensions. Some will feel central to who you are. Others may feel less important right now, and that is okay.

- **Cultural background:** Where your family comes from, traditions you practice
- **Language(s):** The languages you speak and think in
- **Faith or spirituality:** Your beliefs, practices, or questions about meaning
- **Gender:** How you understand and express your gender
- **Interests and passions:** What you love to do, learn about, or create
- **Values:** What matters most to you, honesty, loyalty, justice, creativity
- **Relationships:** How you connect with family, friends, and community
- **Future self:** Who you want to become

### Reflection Questions

1. Which three dimensions feel most central to who you are right now?
2. Which dimension do you wish others understood better about you?
3. Is there a part of your identity that feels in conflict with another part?

## Section 2: The Pressure to Be One Thing

### External Expectations

You may feel pressure from different directions:

- Family expects you to be a certain way
- Friends expect you to fit in
- Social media shows you who you should be
- Culture tells you what success looks like

### The Truth About Complexity

You can be more than one thing at once. You can love your culture and question some of its traditions. You can be serious about your grades and also love video games. You can feel confident in some areas and uncertain in others.

Complexity is not confusion. It is depth.

## Section 3: Mapping Your Identity

### Exercise: My Identity Map

Take a blank piece of paper. Draw a circle in the center and write your name. Then draw lines outward to smaller circles, each representing a dimension of your identity.

For each circle, write:

- **What it means to me now**
- **What I love about this part of me**
- **What feels challenging about it**
- **How this might evolve as I grow**

### Exercise: Three Versions of Me

Write a short paragraph (3-5 sentences) for each:

1. **Who I am when I am with my family**
2. **Who I am when I am with my friends**
3. **Who I am when I am alone**

Now reflect: Are these three versions very different? What stays the same across all three? The parts that stay the same are often your core self.

## Section 4: When Identity Feels Hard

### It Is Normal To

- Not have everything figured out
- Change your mind about what matters to you
- Feel different from the people around you
- Grieve a version of yourself you are outgrowing
- Feel pulled between two cultures, two languages, or two worlds

### When to Seek Support

If you are feeling lost, disconnected from yourself, or if questions about identity are causing you real distress, talking to someone can help. A counselor can provide a safe space to explore these questions without judgment.
