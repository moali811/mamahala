## Section 1: Emotional Literacy

### Naming Your Emotions

Most people operate with about five emotion words: happy, sad, angry, anxious, and fine. But emotional literacy means having a richer vocabulary. The more precisely you can name what you feel, the better you can manage it.

### The Emotion Spectrum

Instead of just "sad," you might be feeling:

- Disappointed
- Grieving
- Lonely
- Nostalgic
- Hopeless
- Melancholic

Instead of just "angry," you might be feeling:

- Frustrated
- Resentful
- Betrayed
- Disrespected
- Overwhelmed
- Protective

### Practice

Three times a day, pause and ask yourself: "What am I feeling right now?" Try to get more specific than the basic five. This simple practice builds emotional awareness over time.

## Section 2: Boundaries Without Guilt

### What Boundaries Are

Boundaries are not walls. They are guidelines that protect your energy, your time, and your wellbeing. They tell the world how you want to be treated. They are not selfish. They are necessary.

### Common Boundary Situations for Young Adults

- Saying no to social plans when you need rest
- Telling a family member you cannot lend them money
- Setting work hours and not responding to emails at midnight
- Declining emotional labor you do not have capacity for
- Limiting time with people who drain you

### The Boundary Script

"I care about you, and I need to be honest. [State the boundary]. This is not about you doing something wrong. It is about what I need to be okay."

### Why It Feels Hard

If you grew up in a family where boundaries were not modeled or respected, setting them as an adult will feel uncomfortable. That discomfort is not a sign that you are doing something wrong. It is a sign that you are doing something new.

## Section 3: Asking for Help

### Why It Feels Hard

Asking for help can feel like admitting failure, especially if you grew up being the capable one, the independent one, or the one everyone else relied on.

### The Reframe

Asking for help is not a sign of weakness. It is a social skill and a survival strategy. No one builds a meaningful life entirely alone.

### How to Ask

1. **Be specific.** Instead of "I need help," try "Can you help me review my resume this week?"
2. **Choose the right person.** Not everyone is equipped to help with every need.
3. **Accept the help.** When someone shows up for you, let them. Do not minimize it or immediately try to pay them back.

## Section 4: Sitting With Discomfort

### The Avoidance Trap

Most of us spend enormous energy avoiding uncomfortable emotions. We scroll, we binge-watch, we over-work, we over-eat, we over-plan. These are all strategies to not feel what we are feeling.

### The Alternative

What if, instead of avoiding the discomfort, you just sat with it? Not forever. Not without tools. But for five minutes, you allowed yourself to feel what is actually there.

### The RAIN Technique

- **R — Recognize** what you are feeling. Name it.
- **A — Allow** it to be there. Do not push it away.
- **I — Investigate** with curiosity. Where do you feel it in your body? What triggered it?
- **N — Nurture** yourself. What would you say to a friend feeling this way? Say that to yourself.

## Section 5: Building Your Emotional Toolkit

### Daily Practices

- **Morning check-in:** How am I feeling? What do I need today?
- **Evening reflection:** What went well? What was hard? What am I grateful for?
- **Weekly boundary audit:** Did I say yes when I meant no? Where do I need to set a limit?

### When to Seek Support

If you are consistently struggling with emotional regulation, boundaries, or self-worth, professional support can accelerate your growth. You do not need to be in crisis to see a counselor.
