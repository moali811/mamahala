## Section 1: Why You Fight the Way You Fight

Before you can change how you argue, you need to understand what drives your conflict style.

### The Four Conflict Patterns

Most couples fall into one of four patterns:

- **Pursue-Withdraw**: One partner chases the conversation while the other retreats. The pursuer feels abandoned. The withdrawer feels overwhelmed. Both feel unheard.
- **Escalate-Escalate**: Both partners match each other's intensity. Voices rise. Words get sharper. The original issue disappears under layers of accusation.
- **Avoid-Avoid**: Both partners sidestep difficult topics. The house is peaceful but the emotional distance grows. Resentment simmers quietly.
- **Demand-Comply**: One partner makes the rules. The other follows to keep the peace. One feels controlled. The other feels unsupported.

### Your Conflict Audit

Answer these questions honestly:

1. When we disagree, I tend to: (a) push for resolution immediately, (b) need space first, (c) avoid it entirely, (d) give in to end it
2. My partner tends to: (same options)
3. After an argument, I feel: (a) drained, (b) anxious, (c) disconnected, (d) relieved it is over
4. The topic we avoid most is: _______________

## Section 2: The Fair Fighting Framework

These five rules transform destructive arguments into productive conversations.

### Rule 1: The 24-Hour Cool-Down

If emotions are above a 7 out of 10, stop. Say: "I need to pause this conversation. I care about this topic and I want to discuss it well. Can we come back to it in [specific time]?"

The key: name a specific return time. "Later" feels like avoidance. "Tomorrow at 8pm after the kids are in bed" feels like commitment.

### Rule 2: Start with "I" Not "You"

Replace: "You never help around the house."
With: "I feel overwhelmed when I handle the housework alone. I need us to find a system that works for both of us."

The formula: "I feel [emotion] when [specific situation]. I need [specific request]."

### Rule 3: One Topic at a Time

Arguments spiral when past grievances pile on. If the conversation started about dishes, stay on dishes. Write down other topics that surface and agree to address them separately.

### Rule 4: Repair Attempts

A repair attempt is any effort to de-escalate during conflict. Research shows that the success of repair attempts, not the absence of conflict, predicts relationship satisfaction.

Examples of repair attempts:
- "Can I start over? I did not say that well."
- "I can see this is important to you."
- A gentle touch on the arm
- "We are on the same team here."
- Humor (when appropriate and not dismissive)

### Rule 5: The Soft Landing

End every difficult conversation with a reconnection gesture. This could be: a hug, a summary of what you heard, an acknowledgment of what was hard, or simply "Thank you for talking about this with me."

## Section 3: The Repair Conversation Template

Use this template after an argument that went badly.

**Step 1: Acknowledge** (2 minutes each, no interruptions)
"When we argued about ___, I think I hurt you when I ___. That was not okay."

**Step 2: Listen** (2 minutes each)
"What I heard you say is ___. Is that right?"

**Step 3: Own** (1 minute each)
"My part in this was ___."

**Step 4: Request** (1 minute each)
"Next time, it would help me if you could ___."

**Step 5: Reconnect**
Physical touch, a shared activity, or simply sitting together in silence.

## Section 4: Building a Conflict-Resilient Relationship

### The Weekly Check-In

Set a weekly 20-minute check-in (not during conflict) to discuss:
- One thing your partner did this week that you appreciated
- One thing you are struggling with (not about your partner)
- One thing you would like to request for the coming week

### Knowing When to Get Help

Consider couples counseling if:
- The same argument repeats without resolution
- You have stopped trying to repair
- Contempt (eye-rolling, sarcasm, dismissiveness) has become regular
- One or both partners feel emotionally unsafe
- You are reading this and thinking "it is too late for us" (it probably is not, but that feeling is a signal)
