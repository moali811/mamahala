## Section 1: Recognizing Burnout

### The Three Dimensions of Burnout

1. **Exhaustion:** Physical, emotional, and mental depletion. You feel tired even after resting.
2. **Cynicism:** Detachment and negativity toward your studies, activities, or relationships. Things that used to excite you now feel meaningless.
3. **Reduced efficacy:** The feeling that nothing you do makes a difference. Your confidence in your own abilities drops.

### Burnout Self-Assessment

Rate each statement from 1 (never) to 5 (always):

**Physical Signs**
- I feel physically exhausted most days ___
- I get sick more often than usual (colds, headaches, stomach issues) ___
- I have trouble sleeping, or I sleep too much and still feel tired ___
- My appetite has changed significantly (eating too much or too little) ___
- I rely on caffeine, sugar, or other stimulants to get through the day ___

**Emotional Signs**
- I feel emotionally numb or disconnected ___
- I dread going to class or doing schoolwork ___
- I feel irritable or short-tempered with people I care about ___
- I feel like crying for no clear reason ___
- I feel anxious even about small tasks ___

**Cognitive and Behavioral Signs**
- I have trouble concentrating even on things I enjoy ___
- I have withdrawn from friends or activities ___
- I feel like my efforts do not matter ___
- I procrastinate on things I used to handle easily ___
- I cannot remember the last time I felt truly rested ___

**Scoring:**

- **15-30:** Mild to moderate stress. You are not in burnout yet, but you are heading in that direction. Now is the time to intervene.
- **31-50:** Warning zone. You are showing clear signs of burnout. Please take this seriously and begin the recovery steps below.
- **51-75:** Burnout territory. Please seek support from a counselor, trusted adult, or campus wellness service. You do not have to do this alone.

## Section 2: Understanding Your Burnout

### Common Causes for Students

- Academic overload without adequate breaks
- Perfectionism and fear of failure
- Lack of social support or meaningful connection
- Financial stress or working while studying
- Family expectations and cultural pressure
- Comparison with high-achieving peers
- Loss of autonomy, feeling like your life is not your own
- Taking on too many extracurriculars to build a resume
- A difficult home environment that drains your energy before the school day even starts

### The Burnout Equation

Burnout = High Demands + Low Resources + Low Recovery

To recover, you need to address all three parts: reduce demands where possible, increase your resources and support, and prioritize recovery.

## Section 3: The Recovery Plan

### Week 1-2: Emergency Measures

These are immediate actions to stop the bleeding:

1. **Cancel one thing.** Look at your schedule and remove the lowest-priority commitment. Give yourself permission. Write it here: I am letting go of _______________________________________________ this week.
2. **Set a hard stop time each night.** No studying after this hour. Non-negotiable. My stop time is _______________.
3. **Sleep.** This is not optional. Aim for 7-8 hours. Burnout recovery starts with rest. Set a bedtime alarm on your phone.
4. **Move your body gently.** A 15-minute walk. Stretching. Yoga. Nothing intense. The goal is to get out of your head and into your body.
5. **Reach out to one person.** Tell someone you trust how you are feeling. You do not need to have it all figured out. Just say: "I am struggling and I wanted someone to know."

**Week 1-2 Check-in:**
- Did I cancel or reduce one commitment? ___
- Am I honoring my stop time most nights? ___
- How is my sleep? (1 = terrible, 5 = restful): ___
- Did I move my body at least 3 times this week? ___
- Did I talk to someone about how I am feeling? ___

### Week 3-4: Rebuilding

1. **Audit your commitments.** What can you drop, delegate, or postpone? Use the energy audit above to guide you.
2. **Reintroduce one enjoyable activity.** Something that is not productive, just pleasurable. Drawing, cooking, watching a movie, playing a game. Write it here: _______________________________________________.
3. **Create a sustainable study schedule.** One that includes breaks, buffer time, and days off. If your old schedule burned you out, the answer is not a better version of the same schedule. It is a fundamentally different approach.
4. **Practice saying no.** Start small. Build the muscle. Here is a script: "Thank you for thinking of me. I do not have the capacity for that right now."
5. **Eat regular meals.** Burnout recovery requires fuel. If cooking feels like too much, keep simple options on hand: fruit, nuts, yogurt, bread and cheese. Do not let perfect be the enemy of fed.

**Week 3-4 Check-in:**
- What commitment did I drop or reduce? _______________________________________________
- What enjoyable activity did I bring back? _______________________________________________
- Did I say no to something this week? ___
- Am I eating at least two proper meals a day? ___

### Month 2: Rebuilding Your Foundation

By now, the emergency phase should be stabilizing. This month is about building a foundation that prevents you from ending up here again.

1. **Identify your warning signs.** What are the first signals that you are heading toward burnout? Write down your personal early warning signs so you can catch it next time.
   - My first warning sign is: _______________________________________________
   - My second warning sign is: _______________________________________________
   - My third warning sign is: _______________________________________________

2. **Build recovery into your routine,** not as an afterthought but as a priority. Schedule it the same way you schedule classes and study time.

3. **Set boundaries around your time and energy.** This might mean saying no to social obligations, limiting how much you check email, or telling a professor you need an extension.

4. **Regularly check in with yourself:** "How am I doing, really?" Not how you are performing, but how you are feeling.

5. **Maintain your support network.** Burnout thrives in isolation. Keep talking to the people who know what you are going through.

### Month 3+: Sustainable Habits

1. **Weekly check-in ritual.** Every Sunday, spend 10 minutes asking yourself: What drained me this week? What restored me? What do I need next week?
2. **Monthly boundary review.** Are the boundaries I set still holding? Have new demands crept in?
3. **Watch for relapse signs** and intervene early. Burnout has a pattern of returning if the underlying conditions do not change.
4. **Celebrate your recovery.** You did something hard. Acknowledge it.

## Section 4: Preventing Future Burnout

### The Sustainable Success Model

Success is not about grinding until you break. It is about finding a pace you can maintain over the long term. Ask yourself:

- "Can I maintain this pace for the next six months?"
- "Am I sacrificing my health for my grades?"
- "What would I tell a friend who was this exhausted?"
- "Am I doing this because I want to, or because I think I should?"
- "Who am I trying to prove something to?"

### Your Non-Negotiables Template

Write down the things that are non-negotiable for your wellbeing. These are the things you protect even when everything else gets busy. Be specific.

1. _______________________________________________ (example: 7 hours of sleep every night)
2. _______________________________________________ (example: one full day off per week)
3. _______________________________________________ (example: eating three meals a day)
4. _______________________________________________ (example: talking to my best friend at least once a week)
5. _______________________________________________ (example: 20 minutes of movement every day)

Now write down what you will do when these non-negotiables are threatened:

"When I notice I am skipping my non-negotiables, I will _______________________________________________."

"The person I will reach out to for accountability is _______________________________________________."

### The Burnout Prevention Checklist

Review this monthly. If you check more than three of these, it is time to intervene before burnout returns.

- [ ] I have been sleeping less than 7 hours for more than a week
- [ ] I skipped my non-negotiables more than twice this week
- [ ] I feel resentful about my responsibilities
- [ ] I have been canceling social plans to work
- [ ] I am relying on caffeine or sugar to function
- [ ] I feel guilty when I rest
- [ ] I cannot remember the last time I did something just for fun
- [ ] I have been snapping at people I care about
- [ ] I feel like I am falling behind no matter how hard I try
